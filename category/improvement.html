Investing in yourself

When asked about investments, or investing in something, most people think about monetary values like stocks or real estate. Wealth can be found elsewhere, too. A great property to also invest is you!

In the following article I will outline the different currencies of investment, different options, and how they relate to each other.

// ## Definitions: investment, currency Definitions of investment oftentimes focus on the economic aspect^1:

“To commit (money or capital) in order to gain a financial return.”

Generally speaking an investment is any commitment in order to gain something in the future:

  • Commitment ↔ currency ↔ future gain.

One form or currency of commitment is money. No doubts about that. Other forms can be seen as willpower, physical strength, etc. depending on your world views and line of work. But one form is universal, and that is time.

On calculating the worth of an investment

A term often mentioned alongside investments is return of investment (ROI). ROI calculates the relative gain from an investment:

ROI = (gain from investment - cost of investment) / cost of investment

A ROI greater 0 expresses an overall gain of investment, while a ROI smaller 0 represents an overall loss. ROI is often expressed as percentage^3. ROI is a simple metric, and should be adjusted to the individual needs, for example in conjunction with time intervals. Another way to figure out if an investment is good, is to calculate when it amortizes — when it returns its initial cost.

ROI, as well as amortizing, loose value, if the expected return is hard to put in numbers.

  • Paying money to gain time results in the question, what time is worth to somebody.
  • Investing money for knowledge? One could compare a future with or without a job, where this knowledge is needed?
  • Using time to commit to a personal moral/ethic/… point of view? (For example searching and only buying vegan products). It’s personal, there is no objective way to determine its value.

The value of traditional commitments and returns like time and money is relatively easy to determine. Personal, emotional and unreliable commitments and returns are hardly possible to estimate. Common sense, and in some cases (health) statistics are the only possibilities to judge such an investment.

Currency exchange

With our definition of investment, and two forms of commitments / currencies, lets get the obvious out of the way, the conversions / exchanges between money, time and each other:

  • money ↔ money Buying stocks, commodities, real estate, committing to startups, … ^2, selling at higher value
  • time ↔ money Doing work, getting paid
  • money ↔ time Paying others doing work, having time doing something else instead
  • time ↔ time Using time for optimizing workflows, systems, doing more in shorter span of time

There are no clear boundaries (for example doing research for the right financial investments uses time, whereas paying someone else uses money; or better workflows can yield money too), but the general understanding is clear.

ROI can be used to evaluate if the investment is worthwhile, if an investment has an expected outcome:

Spending 100€ to get 50€ back -> ROI = (50 - 100) / 100 = -0.5 -> 50% loss, surely no good investment, while spending 100€ to get 150€ back -> ROI = (150 - 100) / 100 = 0.5 -> 50% win.

The same applies to time. Investing 2 hours to reduce the time for a specific task from 4 to 1 minute seems not worthwhile if the task only runs once a week. It would need 40 weeks to amortize the investment. This changes drastically, if the task runs 3 times a day. The 2 hours invested will yield a positive return in just 2 weeks.
